How JPEG Works

Interactive · Image Processing

Throwing away what your eyes won't miss.

PNG never loses a bit — but on photographs that's expensive, because there's too much unpredictable texture to store exactly. JPEG takes the opposite bet: it's lossy, permanently discarding detail — but it's clever about which detail, deleting the parts human vision barely registers. Done right, you can shrink a photo 10–20× and barely notice. Here's how, on a very good dog.

Step 1

Brightness matters more than color

Your eyes have far more cells for brightness than for color. JPEG exploits this by first splitting the image into one brightness channel (Y) and two color channels (Cb, blue-ish; Cr, red-ish) — a representation called YCbCr. It then stores the color channels at half resolution, which alone roughly halves the data with almost no visible change.

Notice the color channels (Cb, Cr) are smooth and low-detail — exactly why JPEG can afford to store them coarsely.

Step 2

Turning pixels into patterns (the DCT)

JPEG chops each channel into 8×8 pixel blocks and rewrites every block as a recipe: a weighted sum of 64 fixed patterns, from flat (top-left) to increasingly fine stripes and checkers (toward the bottom-right). This is the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT). The 64 patterns are always the same — only the 64 weights change per block:

Where have I seen this before? The DCT is a close cousin of the Fourier transform (the DFT) — both express a signal as a sum of waves. The DFT uses sines and cosines and returns complex numbers; the DCT uses only cosines and stays real-valued, which is tidier for images and packs a typical photo's energy into fewer terms. "Frequency" here just means how fast brightness wiggles across the block.

Top-left is the block's average brightness (the DC term). Moving right and down adds finer detail (the AC terms). The magic: in real photos, almost all the "energy" lands in the top-left few patterns — smooth regions barely use the fine ones at all.

Step 3

Quantization: the lossy knob

Here's where information is actually thrown away. Each of the 64 weights is divided by a number and rounded. The divisors live in a quantization table — small for the important low-frequency patterns, large for the fine high-frequency ones (which the eye forgives). Rounding many small weights down to zero is what makes JPEG tiny, because long runs of zeros compress beautifully.

$\text{stored weight} = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\dfrac{\text{DCT weight}}{\text{quantization step}}\right)$

The quality setting just scales this whole table: high quality → small steps → few zeros → big file; low quality → big steps → mostly zeros → tiny file. You can watch this exact table (grid 3) respond to the quality slider in the block explorer below.

The fewer weights survive (step 3), the less data JPEG stores for that block — but the blockier the rebuilt result. At very low quality, whole blocks collapse to a single flat color, which is exactly the "JPEG look."

Step 6

Where JPEG struggles

JPEG is tuned for photos — smooth gradients, fur, foliage. It's bad at hard edges: sharp text, line art, or logos develop "ringing" (ghostly ripples) and blocky halos, because a crisp edge needs many high-frequency patterns that quantization throws away. That's why screenshots of text should be PNG (lossless), not JPEG. Crank the quality down and look near the dog's sharp fur edges to see it start.

What's next

JPEG's transform-then-quantize recipe is 30+ years old, and modern formats improve on it. WebP beats JPEG by predicting each block from its neighbors before transforming, and by using a stronger entropy coder. The same core idea also powers video (H.264/H.265 do it on moving blocks).
