How 3D Graphics Works

Outline · WIP

From triangles to pixels.

Every 3D game, movie, and CAD model comes down to one question: given a world made of points and triangles, plus a camera, what color is each pixel on screen? This is the outline for an interactive series that builds the answer one stage at a time.

Part 1

What a 3D scene is made of

3D objects are meshes: a list of vertices (points in space, each an $(x,y,z)$ coordinate), connected into triangles (three vertices each). Triangles are the universal building block because they're always flat and always convex, which makes them fast and unambiguous to draw. A smooth-looking sphere is really thousands of tiny triangles.

We'll also need a coordinate convention (which way is up, is $z$ toward or away from you) and the idea of normals — the direction a surface faces — which become essential once we add light.

Part 2

Moving things around: transforms

To place, rotate, and resize objects we multiply their vertices by matrices. Translation, rotation, and scaling each have a matrix form, and chaining them is just matrix multiplication. A neat trick — homogeneous coordinates (adding a 4th coordinate, $w$) — lets us pack translation into a matrix too, so the entire transform is one $4\times4$ matrix.

For example, rotating by angle $\theta$ about the $z$-axis is $$R_z(\theta)=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0\\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}.$$

Part 3

The camera & projection

A camera is just another transform that moves the whole world so the camera sits at the origin looking down one axis. Then we project 3D onto the 2D screen. Perspective projection makes distant things smaller (how our eyes work) by dividing $x$ and $y$ by depth $z$; orthographic projection keeps sizes constant (useful for CAD and 2D-ish games). The region the camera can actually see is a truncated pyramid called the view frustum.

Part 4

Rasterization: filling triangles

Once triangles are flattened to 2D, the GPU figures out which pixels each one covers and fills them in. We'll cover the coverage test (barycentric coordinates), how vertex values like color get smoothly interpolated across a triangle, and the z-buffer — a per-pixel depth record that decides what's in front when triangles overlap.

▦
Demo: rasterizer up close
Drag triangle corners on a pixel grid; watch coverage, interpolated color, and z-buffer occlusion resolve.
Part 5

Light & shading

Flat-colored triangles look like cardboard. Shading computes a color per pixel from the surface's normal and the light direction. We'll build up the classic model: ambient (a base fill), diffuse (Lambert — brightness $\propto \max(0,\ \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{l})$, brightest when the surface faces the light), and specular (shiny highlights). Together that's Phong shading.

☀
Demo: lighting lab
Move the light, dial ambient / diffuse / specular and shininess, see each term's contribution separately.
Part 6

Textures

Real surfaces have detail no mesh could afford in triangles, so we paste 2D images (textures) onto them. Each vertex gets a 2D UV coordinate saying where it lands on the image, and the rasterizer interpolates UVs to look up a color per pixel. We'll also touch on why textures get filtered and mip-mapped to avoid shimmering — and how this connects back to image compression.

▤

Part 7

The whole pipeline

Putting it together, every frame flows through the same stages: vertices → transform → project → rasterize → shade → pixels. Modern GPUs run this for millions of triangles, sixty-plus times a second. The finale demo will let you watch a single triangle travel the entire pipeline, stage by stage.
